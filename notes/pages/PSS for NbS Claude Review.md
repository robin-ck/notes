- **# Planning support systems for nature-based solutions: a synthesis of the academic literature**
  
  **The field of Planning Support Systems (PSS) for Nature-Based Solutions (NbS) is rich in tools and frameworks yet plagued by a persistent gap between what gets built and what gets used.** Despite more than 30 dedicated NbS tools catalogued in recent reviews and a rapidly growing evidence base, practitioner adoption remains low, institutional contexts remain misaligned, and data limitations continue to constrain what tools can realistically model. This synthesis draws on approximately 120 academic papers and reports (primarily 2018–2025) to map the current state of PSS for NbS across four domains—urban green infrastructure, flood/water management, biodiversity and ecosystem services, and climate adaptation—and distill the biggest challenges, current developments, and emerging directions that should inform the design of a new PSS tool.
  
  ---
  
  **## The implementation gap remains the field's defining problem**
  
  The most fundamental challenge in PSS for NbS is not technical capability but adoption. **The "implementation gap"—first diagnosed by Vonk, Geertman, and Schot (2005) in *Environment and Planning B***—persists two decades later.**** Geertman and Stillwell (2020, *Environment and Planning B*) note it is "disturbing to observe the extent to which new computer-based support systems are developed by researchers to the point of adoption but are never implemented in planning practice." This gap is not merely an NbS problem; it is endemic to PSS as a field. But NbS planning adds layers of complexity that make adoption even harder.
  
  The barriers cluster into four reinforcing categories. First, **technical and design barriers**: tools are often overly complex, data-hungry, and narrowly specialized. Lee's (1973) "seven sins of large-scale models"—hyper-comprehensiveness, complicatedness, data hungriness, mechanicalness—remain relevant, as Kuller, Farrelly, Deletic, and Bach (2018, *Environmental Science & Policy*) found through 24 interviews with water-sensitive urban design practitioners in Melbourne. Most existing NbS tools focus on a single ecosystem service at a single scale (Sarabi et al., 2022, *Sustainable Cities and Society*), while practitioners need holistic, multiscale assessments. Russo, Lanzilotti, Costabile, and Pettit (2018, *Computers, Environment and Urban Systems*) confirmed through user testing with professional planners that major usability problems persist—the way functionality is provided through the user interface matters as much as the functionality itself.
  
  Second, **organizational and institutional barriers** block uptake even when tools are well-designed. Siloed departmental structures in municipal governments mean NbS—which are inherently cross-sectoral—lack a natural institutional home (Mendes et al., 2023, *Ecology & Society*; Hölscher et al., 2023, *npj Urban Sustainability*). Managers resist adopting unfamiliar technologies with unpredictable organizational consequences (Jiang, Geertman, and Witte, 2020, *Environment and Planning B*). Path dependency toward grey infrastructure runs deep: established engineering standards, procurement frameworks, and performance metrics all favor conventional solutions (Seddon et al., 2020, *Philosophical Transactions of the Royal Society B*; Martin et al., 2025, *Journal of Environmental Management*).
  
  Third, **capacity and knowledge barriers** limit who can use these tools. Many planners lack GIS expertise, and the NbS concept itself remains poorly understood among practitioners—Brillinger et al. (2021, *Ambio*) found NbS is "little known among German planning practitioners." Staff turnover erodes institutional memory of tools already adopted. In the Global South, these barriers intensify: Kuller et al. (2022, *Cities*) found that Indonesian urban planners face inadequate spatial data, limited local government capacity, and political contexts that further constrain tool use.
  
  Fourth, and perhaps most critically, **the development process itself is misaligned**. Te Brömmelstroet and Schrijnen (2010, *Environment and Planning B*) identified the central weakness as a lack of communication between developers focused on technical sophistication and potential users embedded in planning practice. Tools are **"technology-push" rather than "demand-pull."** Vonk and Geertman (2008, *Applied Spatial Analysis and Policy*) described a vicious circle: low adoption yields few lessons, which yields poor improvement, which perpetuates low adoption.

  **## What planners actually need diverges from what tools provide**
  
  Empirical studies of practitioner needs reveal a clear pattern. Pelzer, Arciniegas, Geertman, and Lenferink (2014, *Computers, Environment and Urban Systems*) found that practitioners value **communication, collaboration, and learning** as the most important functions of PSS—not sophisticated analytical outputs. Kuller et al. (2018) found that green infrastructure planning in practice is "opportunistic rather than strategic," meaning tools must fit ad hoc, context-specific decision moments rather than idealized linear planning processes.
  
  The literature converges on several core practitioner requirements for NbS-PSS tools:
- **Rapid, easy-to-understand outputs** that facilitate dialogue among non-technical stakeholders (Radhakrishnan et al., 2021, *Sustainable Cities and Society*)
- **Alignment with existing workflows and software** rather than standalone platforms requiring new skills (Kuller et al., 2018; GOAT study, Pajares et al., ~2022)
- **Multi-scale capability** spanning site, neighborhood, and city scales within one framework (Sarabi et al., 2022)
- **Integration of biophysical, socio-economic, and governance dimensions** rather than single-domain technical analysis (Kuller et al., 2018; Kabisch, Frantzeskaki, and Hansen, 2022, *Ambio*)
- **Transparency** in underlying models and assumptions so outputs can be interrogated and trusted (te Brömmelstroet, 2012, *Computers, Environment and Urban Systems*)
  
  Pelzer (2017, *Transportation Research Part A*) makes an essential distinction: **usability is necessary but never sufficient**. A tool can be extremely user-friendly yet still not useful if it does not fit the planning task. "Task-technology fit"—the match between what the tool does and what the planning situation demands—is the deeper determinant of value. This finding has direct design implications: tools should be designed around planning tasks and decision moments, not around technical capabilities.

  **## Governance contexts shape what tools can realistically accomplish**
  
  Voskamp et al. (2021, *Sustainability*) produced perhaps the most actionable finding for tool designers: among 31+ NbS tools reviewed, the vast majority address expertise and knowledge gaps, while **far fewer address institutional barriers (13 tools), resource constraints (11), or collaborative governance challenges (10)**. This reveals a systematic oversupply of technical tools and undersupply of governance-aware tools—a mismatch that helps explain persistent low adoption.
  
  The governance literature highlights several structural tensions that any PSS must navigate. NbS require holistic, cross-sectoral planning, but municipal governance remains organized in departmental silos. Gonçalves et al. (2022, *Sustainable Cities and Society*) note explicitly that the collaborative NBS planning approach "is usually not aligned with the mainstream sectoral planning process in cities." Dorst et al. (2022, *Landscape and Urban Planning*), working within the NATURVATION project across six European countries, found that **similar barriers arise from different structural conditions** in different contexts—limited citizen engagement stems from low environmental awareness in Spain, lack of resources in Hungary, and NIMBY-ism in the Netherlands. This means one-size-fits-all tools will systematically fail; context-assessment capabilities must be built in.
  
  Frantzeskaki (2019, *Environmental Science and Policy*) distilled seven lessons from 15 urban NbS experiments across 11 European cities, emphasizing that NbS must be aesthetically pleasing for citizen acceptance, require cross-departmental coordination and political champions, and need financial innovation. Hölscher et al. (2023, *npj Urban Sustainability*) identified three mainstreaming strategies from ten European cities: institutionalizing a systems approach across departmental silos, fostering inclusive collaborations for localized interventions, and building reflexivity through iterative learning. **Hard-to-overcome barriers include opportunistic rather than consistent political support, short-term procurement frameworks emphasizing costs over benefits, and insufficient staffing.**
  
  A provocative finding from Wamsler and Alkan-Olsson (2020, *Climatic Change*) warns that participatory features in tools must be carefully designed: under current conditions, citizen engagement in NbS planning **can actually hamper sustainable outcomes** when municipalities lack capacity for meaningful participation and citizens have conflicting personal interests. Tools with participatory features need governance scaffolding—trained facilitators, structured processes—to avoid tokenistic engagement that produces worse results than expert-led planning.

  **## Data and indicator availability constrain the boundaries of what is possible**
  
  The progression of indicator frameworks—from the **EKLIPSE framework** (Raymond et al., 2017) to the **EU NbS Impact Assessment Handbook** (Dumitru and Wendling, 2021, covering 12 societal challenge areas with contributions from 17 Horizon 2020 projects) to the **IUCN Global Standard** (2020, with 8 criteria and 28 indicators)—represents real progress toward standardization. But a fundamental gap persists between what these frameworks recommend measuring and what data is actually available for operational planning tools.
  
  Chausson et al. (2020, *Global Change Biology*) produced the first global systematic map of NbS effectiveness evidence (386 studies), revealing severe biases: **58% of studies came from high-income nations; only 5% from low-income countries**. Cost-effectiveness data comparing NbS with grey alternatives was scarce. Just 16% of studies included social or economic outcome measures—the rest relied on biophysical metrics alone. This matters profoundly for PSS design, because decision-makers demand economic comparisons that the evidence base cannot yet support.
  
  Several structural data challenges constrain PSS capabilities. **Scale mismatch** is endemic: city-level vulnerability data exists from global datasets and remote sensing, but site-level implementation data (soil conditions, micro-topography, existing infrastructure capacity) is frequently unavailable. The NBS-PSS developed by Sarabi et al. (2022) addresses this through a hierarchical multi-scale approach, but data availability drops sharply at finer planning scales. **Temporal dynamics** are poorly captured: NbS performance changes as ecosystems mature, across seasons, and under shifting climate conditions, yet most tools provide static snapshots. Seddon (2022, *Science*) notes that ecosystem responses to NbS interventions "are much harder to predict and cost than engineered/grey infrastructure."
  
  **Cross-domain indicator standardization remains elusive.** Biodiversity assessment uses ecological metrics, flood risk uses hydrological models, urban heat uses land surface temperature, and ecosystem services valuation uses economic methods—these operate in separate methodological silos. As one review noted, "as long as the benefits of nature-inspired solutions will be classified and valued as 'separated silos,' application of NBS will be challenged" (~2022, *Science of the Total Environment*). Ruangpan et al. (2020, *Natural Hazards and Earth System Sciences*) found that models capable of assessing multiple NbS benefits simultaneously "are not readily available," and existing tools rely on methodologies developed for other purposes.
  
  Remote sensing and earth observation offer partial solutions. Chrysoulakis et al. (2021, *Remote Sensing*) demonstrated that spaceborne sensing can monitor NbS performance continuously, and integration with platforms like Google Earth Engine enables cloud-based analysis. But EO cannot capture all NbS benefits—social, cultural, and fine-grained ecological values require in-situ measurement, citizen science data, and ground-truthing.

  **## Current tools and frameworks span a wide but fragmented landscape**
  
  The existing tool ecosystem is extensive but fragmented. The most significant current tools and frameworks, organized by function:
  
  **Purpose-built NbS planning support systems** represent the frontier of integrated tool development. The **NBS-PSS** (Sarabi et al., 2022, *Sustainable Cities and Society*), developed at Eindhoven University of Technology, is the first dedicated PSS with a multiscale hierarchical framework for NbS site and solution prioritization, tested in Eindhoven. The **Adaptation Support Tool** (van de Ven et al., 2016) uses MapTable-based interaction for blue-green adaptation planning in Dutch cities. **SSANTO** (Kuller et al., 2019, *Science of the Total Environment*) provides rapid GIS-based multi-criteria suitability mapping for water-sensitive urban design in Melbourne. The **GIUR-PSS** (~2021, PubMed Central) uses fuzzy comprehensive evaluation for green infrastructure urban resilience assessment, tested in Ohio.
  
  **Ecosystem services assessment platforms** are the most mature tool category. **InVEST** (Natural Capital Project, Stanford), documented by Hamel et al. (2021, *npj Urban Sustainability*), offers 18+ open-source models including urban-specific modules for cooling, flood risk, stormwater, and nature access—used globally in data-rich and data-scarce contexts. **i-Tree** (USDA Forest Service) quantifies urban tree ecosystem services and has been applied in 130+ countries (Davies et al., 2019, *Landscape and Urban Planning*), though it is limited to tree-related services. **ARIES** (Basque Center for Climate Change) stands out as a genuinely operational AI-powered semantic web platform with approximately 6,000 active users across five continents, automating ecosystem service assessments. **LUCI** (Victoria University of Wellington) and the new **EnhancES** (2025, *Ecosystem Services*), an open-source QGIS toolbox, expand options for spatial planning integration.
  
  **MCDA tools** for NbS selection have proliferated. Bousquet et al. (2023, *Blue-Green Systems*) systematically reviewed 124 MCDA-NBS papers, finding tools are rarely accessible or adaptable across contexts and remain concentrated in the Global North. Mozumdar and Cinelli (2025/2026, *Frontiers in Sustainability*) identified **Urban GreenUP**, **NbS Simulation Visualisation Tool**, and **UrbanProof Toolkit** as particularly well-suited for NbS co-creation. Venter et al. (2021, *Ecosystem Services*) developed a notable cloud-based MCDA tool using Google Earth Engine for green roof spatial prioritization in Oslo.
  
  **Assessment and evaluation frameworks** provide the conceptual architecture that tools should implement. The **EKLIPSE framework** (Raymond et al., 2017) remains foundational, organized around ten societal challenge areas with scale-specific indicators. The **EU NbS Impact Assessment Handbook** (Dumitru and Wendling, 2021) expanded this with 12 challenge areas and detailed methodological guidance from 17 Horizon 2020 projects. The **IUCN Global Standard** (2020) provides 8 criteria and 28 indicators with a web-based self-assessment tool.

  **## Where the field is heading: convergence, AI, and digital twins**
  
  The most promising emerging direction is the **convergence of digital twins, AI/ML, participatory tools, and IoT monitoring** into integrated platforms. Several threads are advancing from concept to operational prototype.
  
  **AI and machine learning** have moved from conceptual to demonstrable. Deep learning surrogates for InVEST ecosystem service models (2025, ScienceDirect, applied in Baltimore) overcome the computational bottleneck of spatially explicit optimization, enabling real-time green infrastructure placement across city scales—previously computationally prohibitive. Richards et al. (2024, *People and Nature*) demonstrated generative AI (DALL-E 2 and ChatGPT) for NbS landscape visualization and farm-level advisory in New Zealand, though trust barriers remain significant. A hybrid expert system combining neural networks with case-based reasoning (NBS-CBS, 2022, *Journal of Environmental Management*) offers explainable AI for NbS planning—combining data-driven learning with interpretable reasoning. Bibliometric analysis (2025, *MDPI Atmosphere*) shows AI-NBS research growing exponentially (535 papers, 2011–2024, R²=0.95).
  
  **Digital twins for NbS** have progressed from aspirational to operational in specific domains. The **VARCITIES** project launched digital twins across seven EU pilot cities with real-time NbS monitoring and health/wellbeing dashboards in 2024. A Leuven case study (2025, *MDPI Smart Cities*) demonstrated a streamlined digital twin framework within the Intelligent Communities Lifecycle platform, combining automated urban geometry import, weather data integration, and NbS thermal comfort simulation in a cloud-based participatory interface—**temporary NbS interventions reduced median thermal comfort stress by approximately 2.3°C during extreme heat**. Brasil et al. (2022, *Journal of Hydroinformatics*) provide an important framing contribution, noting that unlike built infrastructure, NbS involve biological processes (soil dynamics, vegetation growth, clogging) that make digital twin modeling fundamentally more complex.
  
  **Geodesign** has established an active research community centered on NbS applications. Adem Esmail et al. (2025, *Landscape and Urban Planning*; 2024, *Ambio*) developed replicable geodesign protocols for transformative NbS planning in Stockholm, finding the approach enabled participants to develop plans balancing housing development with biodiversity but that participants remained skeptical about implementation plausibility. Gottwald et al. (2021, *Ambio*) applied geodesign to river landscape NbS in Germany. The emerging convergence with AI—geodesign augmented by pattern recognition, scenario optimization, and predictive analytics (2025, *Frontiers of Urban and Rural Planning*)—is genuinely new but still conceptual.
  
  **Participatory and co-design tools** have matured considerably with operational implementations. Pueyo-Ros et al. (2023, *Nature-Based Solutions*) developed a serious game framework for participatory NbS planning using the Tygron platform with real-time indicator feedback. The INTERLACE project's Unlimited Cities app enables citizens to create digital collages of NbS solutions on photographs of their neighborhoods, deployed in Quito and European cities. Dushkova et al. (2023) produced a systematic seven-step co-creation pathway with toolboxes covering co-assessment, co-design, co-implementation, and co-monitoring phases. The "Internet of Nature" framework (Galle et al., 2019) proposes connecting IoT infrastructure with urban ecosystems for "ecosystem intelligence," with pilot implementations including Boston's TreeTect AI-powered tree inventory and Madrid's Greemta project, though urban-scale sensor deployments remain limited.
  
  Several directions have been discussed for years with limited operational progress. **VR/AR for NbS visualization** remains "in infancy" despite discussion since approximately 2018. **Climate model-NbS tool integration**—where climate projection outputs directly drive NbS scenario planning—is consistently identified as critical but rarely achieved in practice; most tools either use static climate projections or provide climate information separate from planning functionality. **IoT sensor networks for NbS monitoring at urban scale** remain at pilot stage despite conceptual maturity.

  **## Implications for designing a new PSS tool for NbS planning**
  
  The literature points toward several design principles that a new PSS tool for NbS should embody to avoid repeating the field's persistent failures.
  
  **Start from planning tasks, not technical capabilities.** The most robust finding across two decades of PSS research is that technology-push development fails. The tool should be designed around specific decision moments in NbS planning workflows—site identification, solution selection, stakeholder negotiation, impact assessment, monitoring—rather than around modeling capabilities. Pelzer's (2017) task-technology fit framework provides an operationalizable design lens.
  
  **Address governance and institutional barriers, not just knowledge gaps.** Voskamp et al.'s (2021) finding that tools overwhelmingly address expertise while neglecting governance is actionable: a new tool should explicitly support cross-departmental collaboration, provide economic comparison with grey alternatives to counter path dependency, and include governance readiness assessment features. It should help navigate the sectoral silos that Mendes et al. (2023) and Hölscher et al. (2023) document as the primary structural barrier.
  
  **Design for multiple scales with graceful data degradation.** The hierarchical approach of the NBS-PSS (Sarabi et al., 2022) is the right architecture: city-scale screening using widely available data (remote sensing, global datasets), neighborhood-scale prioritization using moderate-resolution data, and site-scale design using local data where available. Critically, the tool should function meaningfully even with incomplete data, providing useful outputs at coarser scales while flagging where better data would improve decisions.
  
  **Prioritize communication value over analytical precision.** Pelzer et al. (2014) consistently found practitioners value PSS as communication and collaboration facilitators. The tool should produce outputs that non-technical stakeholders can understand and debate. Visualization capabilities—whether 3D digital twin renderings, serious game interfaces, or simpler map-based dashboards—should be treated as core functionality, not add-ons.
  
  **Build for integration, not replacement.** The fragmented tool landscape means a new PSS should interoperate with existing tools (InVEST, i-Tree, SWMM) rather than attempting to replicate their capabilities. APIs, standard data formats, and compatibility with common GIS platforms (QGIS, ArcGIS) are essential. The ARIES platform's semantic web approach to model and data interoperability offers a design model worth studying.
  
  **Embed uncertainty communication.** Unlike grey infrastructure, NbS performance is inherently uncertain—dependent on ecosystem maturation, climate trajectories, maintenance regimes, and biological complexity. Tools that ignore this uncertainty lose credibility with experienced practitioners. Sensitivity analysis, scenario comparison, and explicit uncertainty ranges should be default features. The MCDA sensitivity testing by the Urban GreenUP project found rankings were "relatively insensitive" (rs > 0.75) across 11 uncertainty types, suggesting that well-designed tools can be robust even with imperfect data—a finding that should be communicated to users to build confidence.
  
  **Plan for sustained adoption beyond the project lifecycle.** Many EU-funded NbS tools disappear when project funding ends. Sustainability strategies—open-source development, integration with institutional workflows, training programs, communities of practice (as recommended by Vonk and Geertman, 2008)—should be designed from the outset, not as afterthoughts.
  
  **## Conclusion**
  
  The PSS-NbS field sits at an inflection point. The theoretical and empirical foundations are mature: we know what barriers exist (institutional silos, data gaps, usability failures, path dependency toward grey infrastructure), what practitioners need (workflow-aligned, multi-scale, communication-focused, governance-aware tools), and where technology is heading (AI-augmented analysis, digital twins, cloud platforms, participatory co-design). The critical challenge is no longer producing another tool but **producing a tool that actually gets used**. This requires inverting the dominant development paradigm: starting from governance contexts and planning tasks rather than from technical capabilities, designing for graceful data degradation rather than data-ideal scenarios, and embedding the tool within institutional relationships rather than delivering it as a standalone product. The most impactful contribution a new PSS could make would be to bridge the persistent divide between NbS multifunctionality and sectoral planning realities—providing the cross-departmental evidence base, economic comparison with grey alternatives, and stakeholder communication capacity that current tools largely fail to deliver.
