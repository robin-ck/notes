topic:: NbS
- Source: Cohen-Shacham et al. (2016), Table 4 and Chapter 3
- NbS is an **umbrella concept** — the approaches below all address societal challenges through ecosystem-based means
- ## 1. Ecosystem Restoration Approaches
	- ### Ecological Restoration
		- Process of **assisting the recovery of an ecosystem** that has been degraded, damaged, or destroyed (Society for Ecological Restoration, 2004)
		- Emerged first as practice, later as research discipline
		- Applied at a wide variety of scales
		- Examples: restoration of a polluted river basin; restoration of a forest degraded by gold mining
	- ### Ecological Engineering
		- Branch of both ecology and engineering; closely linked to ecological restoration ("sister fields")
		- Roots in Odum (1960s) and Ma's work in China (wastewater treatment, recycling, pollution)
		- Can be implemented at all scales — from species to planetary level
		- Examples: self-design of tidal creeks; introduction of plant species for salt marsh restoration; species that trap sediment for coastal protection
	- ### Forest Landscape Restoration (FLR)
		- Process that extends beyond forested ecosystems — focuses on restoring **ecological integrity and human well-being** at the landscape level
		- First described by WWF and IUCN in 2000
		- Reinforced by the **2011 Bonn Challenge** and international conventions (CBD, UNCCD, UNFCCC)
		- Does **not** focus on restoring a pre-existing state — focuses on restoring functionality and regulating service provision
		- Examples: restoration of forests of Shinyanga, Tanzania; Loess Plateau, China
- ## 2. Issue-Specific Ecosystem-Related Approaches
	- ### Ecosystem-based Adaptation (EbA)
		- Framework for addressing the role of **ecosystem services in moderating climate impacts** on people
		- Term coined in 2008; CBD officially defined it in 2009 as an operational tool for climate change adaptation
		- CBD definition: "sustainable management, conservation and restoration of ecosystems, as part of an overall adaptation strategy that takes into account multiple social, economic and cultural co-benefits for local communities"
		- Generally provides benefits on a **local scale**
		- Examples: renaturation of rivers to attenuate flooding; replanting forests with more climate-tolerant species
	- ### Ecosystem-based Mitigation (EbM)
		- Framework to address causes of climate change by **reducing GHG sources or enhancing sinks**
		- Emerged from UNFCCC processes; closely linked to REDD+
		- Emphasises forest ecosystems (afforestation, reforestation, avoided deforestation) and marine/coastal ecosystems (mangroves, peatlands, salt marshes, kelp, seagrass)
		- Aims for **global benefits and long-term effects**
		- Example: restoration of coastal/marine ecosystems for blue carbon storage
	- ### Climate Adaptation Services (CAS)
		- Emerged in scientific literature in 2012 (Jones et al.)
		- Broader than EbA — focuses on **ecological mechanisms that support ecosystems' capacity to adapt** to change
		- Highlights importance of healthy ecosystems to resist, cope with, or transform under climate change impacts
		- Stresses **options and insurance values** of services not currently important but potentially critical in future
	- ### Ecosystem-based Disaster Risk Reduction (Eco-DRR)
		- First mentioned in a 2009 IUCN publication; defined by the Partnership for Environment and Disaster Risk Reduction
		- Focuses on **minimising impacts of hazard events** by enhancing people's capacities to manage and recover
		- More specific than EbA/EbM — focuses on particular hazard events (tsunamis, earthquakes, flooding, cyclones)
		- Also addresses hazards **not linked to climate change** (unlike EbA/EbM)
		- Can be implemented at all scales
		- Examples: restoration of large marshlands to protect from hurricane flooding; use of protected areas to reduce coastal disaster risk
- ## 3. Infrastructure-Related Approaches
	- ### Natural Infrastructure (NI)
		- Restoring **structure, function and composition** of ecosystems to deliver ecosystem services
		- Used at **landscape scale** only
		- Examples: forests for water storage, wetlands for filtration, floodplains for flood attenuation, mangroves for coastal protection
	- ### Green Infrastructure (GI)
		- EU definition: "a strategically planned network of natural and semi-natural areas with other environmental features designed and managed to deliver a wide range of ecosystem services"
		- Used at both **urban and landscape scale**
		- Considers conservation values in land development, growth management and built infrastructure planning
		- Examples: green walls, green roofs, street trees, vegetated drainage basins, urban parks
		- Often involves **hybrid solutions** mixing hard infrastructure with ecosystem-based infrastructure
		- NI and GI share principles of **connectivity, multifunctionality and smart conservation**
- ## 4. Ecosystem-based Management Approaches
	- ### Integrated Coastal Zone Management
		- Ecosystem-based management applied to coastal systems
	- ### Integrated Water Resources Management
		- Ecosystem-based management applied to water resources
	- Derives conceptually and practically from the **Ecosystem Approach**
	- Integrative, **transdisciplinary** focus considering the entire ecosystem including humans
	- Used in scientific research, policy and practice
- ## 5. Ecosystem Protection Approaches
	- ### Area-based conservation approaches including protected area management
	- Most commonly known category — not described in detail in Chapter 3
