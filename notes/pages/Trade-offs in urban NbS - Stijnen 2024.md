- Synthesis of Stijnen, Frantzeskaki and Wijsman (2024), a scoping review of 105 empirical papers on urban NBS trade-offs. They build a typology of trade-offs organised along two axes: **type of trade-off** (rigid / governance / functional) and **planning cycle stage** (planning = OF, usage & maintenance = WITH, across = ACROSS). The whole argument is framed through an **environmental justice (EJ) lens** (distributional, procedural, recognition).
-
- ## Core Argument
	- The NBS literature is obsessed with **co-benefits and win-win framings**, which has created a blind spot on trade-offs. Where trade-offs *are* discussed, they are usually treated in an apolitical way — presented as information to help planners, but without surfacing the conflicts of interest, whose voice counts, or how to actually deal with them.
	- The authors push back: urban planning trade-offs are **inevitable** (following Bush & Doyon 2019, Nóblega-Carriquiry et al. 2022), and some are also **produced by governance practices themselves** (Osborne 2015). So the agenda should shift from *overcoming* trade-offs to **explicitly and accountably dealing with them for a just and sustainable future**.
	- They do this by running an EJ lens (distributional / procedural / recognition, after Schlosberg 2007) across every trade-off category — making visible the political question **"solutions for whom?"**
	- The contribution is three-fold: (1) extend the conceptualisation of NBS trade-offs beyond the ecosystem-services frame, (2) build an empirical inventory of trade-offs from 105 papers, and (3) politicise the discussion through EJ.
-
- ## How Trade-offs Are Conceptualised in the Literature
	- Only **25 of 105 papers** use the word "trade-off" explicitly; **80 address them implicitly** through related concepts.
	- The classical **ES-framed definition** (Haase et al. 2012): a trade-off is "a win-lose or lose-win situation that involves losing one ecosystem service in exchange for gaining another". Closely related: ecosystem **disservices** (Shackleton et al. 2016).
	- Implicit framings across the reviewed papers cluster into:
		- **Disservices** — 16 papers discuss disservices (allergenicity, mosquitoes/hornets, perceived safety, damage from trees) without ever using the word trade-off.
		- **Conflicts** — between values, land uses, user groups, human vs non-human nature.
		- **Tensions** — used interchangeably with conflict; common in governance discussions.
		- **Unintended consequences** — e.g. wetlands becoming ecological traps; littering undermining NBS uptake.
		- **Contestation** — NBS vs development pressure on the same parcel.
		- **Paradoxes** — promised social/ecological benefits vs. lived reality of displacement or exclusion (Ordóñez et al. 2022 on Buen Vivir; Du & Zhang 2020 on UGS distribution).
	- The dominant framing is **stakeholder preferences over ES**, and **biodiversity vs. cultural benefits**. Only Diep et al. (2022) explicitly name *justice* trade-offs.
-
- ## The Typology — Three Groups × Three Stages
	- The paper's novelty is the **2D typology** (their Fig. 3): the type of trade-off × the stage of the planning cycle at which it surfaces.
	- They relabel the stages to emphasise the character of the trade-off in each one:
		- Planning stage → trade-offs **OF** NBS (goal-setting, design)
		- Usage/maintenance stage → trade-offs **WITH** NBS (lived experience once implemented)
		- Multiple stages → trade-offs **ACROSS** the planning cycle
	- The three **groups** capture the *source* of the trade-off:
		- **Rigid** — dilemmas that cannot be resolved; they require a choice. ("Who is the city for?")
		- **Governance** — tensions in how decisions are made and who is included.
		- **Functional** — incompatibilities between the functions the NBS is being asked to provide.
	- Not every cell is populated — rigid trade-offs have no ACROSS manifestation because they are stage-specific dilemmas rather than tensions that carry through the whole cycle.

- ## Sub-categories that don't fit in the table
	- **Governance tensions ACROSS** (the ACROSS row) unpack into three sub-categories:
		- **Centralised vs. decentralised GI governance** — a preference for grey infrastructure, expert-led management and strict rules vs. a preference for distributed GI, collaborative governance and on-site interventions. The choice shapes both spatial footprint and who gets a seat at the decision-making table.
		- **Technical vs. local knowledge systems** — municipal/technical actors prioritise flooding, sanitation, erosion; residents of informal settlements may prioritise odours and safety, sometimes preferring grey over green (Chapa & Hack 2020; Diep et al. 2022). Managing these knowledge bases together is the governance task.
		- **Inclusivity paradoxes** — stewardship arrangements shift labour and cost onto community members (Carmichael et al. 2019; Young & McPherson 2013); early inclusion can drive later rent-rise displacement (Haase et al. 2022); citizen participation can under-represent non-human nature and low-ecological-literacy voices can favour invasive species (Stålhammar 2021).
	- **Recognition dilemmas ACROSS** (the other ACROSS row) also unpack into three:
		- **Restoration vs. social benefits** — urban forest management trades off biodiversity for low-nuisance, shade-providing species (Dickinson & Ramalho 2022); strict conservation restricts social/livelihood uses (Bogotá creek — Rojas Bernal et al. 2022; Cape Town — Stålhammar 2021). The Cape Town case is particularly sharp because of its colonial conservation history.
		- **Regeneration and displacement** — green/blue regeneration repeatedly displaces the residents it claims to serve, documented in informal-settlement river projects (Diep et al. 2022; Douglas 2018; Ordóñez et al. 2022), the Medellín greenbelt (Anguelovski et al. 2018), and the Gowanus canal, NY (Miller 2016).
		- **Multifunctionality conflicts** — pushing NBS to serve many groups can displace existing marginalised user groups, e.g. allotment gardens in Swiss cities transformed into public parks (Tappert et al. 2018), or tensions between users when gardens expand to fend off gentrification (Egerer & Fairbairn 2018).
-
- ## What the Typology Is For
	- Different groups of trade-offs call for **different planning and governance approaches**:
		- **Rigid** trade-offs cannot be designed away — they need **agonistic** planning (Kühn 2021) that opens space for contestation and debate, because the choice itself is the question.
		- **Governance** trade-offs can sometimes be softened through **institutional reform** and context-appropriate process design.
		- **Functional** trade-offs can be addressed through **co-creation** and careful design choices, especially when done with a socio-culturally caring approach.
	- So the typology doesn't just describe — it **prescribes a match between trade-off type and governance mode**. This is the bit most useful for planning-support work.
-
- ## Key Critical Moves
	- **Inclusivity cannot always fix injustices.** An inclusive participatory process may produce procedural justice gains but come at the cost of efficiency, non-human nature, or even (per Haase et al. 2022) distributional injustice downstream when the project drives up rents. This directly challenges the "inclusion = just" equation in the NBS governance literature.
	- **Separation of people and nature** underlies many of the conflicts — framing nature as disservice, or as instrumental to human needs, recurs across categories. The authors nod to ecological/multi-species justice (Pineda-Pinto et al. 2022) as a possible fourth dimension beyond the three-dimensional Schlosberg frame.
	- **Inequitable distribution of NBS itself** — green space is systematically under-provided in vulnerable neighbourhoods — is flagged as a distinct cluster that **falls outside the typology** because it's not a trade-off between incompatible priorities but the result of deliberate allocation choices. Still an EJ issue, but a different one.
-
- ## Methodological & Evidence Notes
	- Scoping review following Arksey & O'Malley (2005), Levac et al. (2010). 1188 hits → 105 included papers after two screenings.
	- Geographic skew: Europe (45), North America (30), Asia (16); only 10 on informal settlements (5 South America, 5 Africa). **Global South underrepresented** — flagged as a gap.
	- **Most trade-offs documented are at the usage/maintenance stage** (42 papers) vs. the planning stage (31). No studies cover the implementation stage alone.
	- **Temporal scale is usually unspecified** (82/105 papers). Perceptions of trade-offs are analysed cross-sectionally without a time reference. Longitudinal work on how trade-off perceptions shift through the NBS life cycle is explicitly called out as a research gap.
	- Only **24/105 papers** explicitly mention justice implications; only **2** use the three Schlosberg dimensions.

- ## Implications for my work
	- This is a useful **counterweight** to the co-benefits framing that dominates the PSS-for-NbS literature I've been reading (see [[PSS for NbS Claude Review]] and [[Highlights - Seddon 2020 NbS]]). Co-benefits and trade-offs are not alternatives — trade-offs are the flip side of multifunctionality and they are **not all technical problems**.
	- For [[MCDA Elements]] and [[GIS-MCDA Overview]]: the typology is basically a diagnostic for **which trade-offs MCDA can and cannot help with**. MCDA is well-suited to **functional** trade-offs (priority-setting, weighting across criteria) and can support some **governance** trade-offs through stakeholder weighting, but it will not resolve **rigid** trade-offs — those need political/agonistic process, not aggregation. Honest framing of MCDA limits matters here.
	- For the PSS I'm designing: a good PSS should make the **type of trade-off explicit** to the user rather than silently aggregating them away, and should flag when a choice is rigid (irreversible, value-laden) vs. functional (optimisable). This links directly to the transparency argument in [[Usefulness of PSS]].
	- The [[Environmental Benefits of Blue-Green Infrastructure]] / [[Social Benefits of Blue-Green Infrastructure]] / [[Economic Benefits of Blue-Green Infrastructure]] split in my own notes maps reasonably onto **functional** trade-offs but does nothing to capture **rigid** and **governance** ones — worth extending.
	- **Justice dimension is the most portable contribution here** — distributional / procedural / recognition gives me vocabulary to use in the PSS framing when I argue why task-technology fit isn't enough: the tool also has to fit a *just* process.
