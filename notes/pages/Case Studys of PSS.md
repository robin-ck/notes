- **Urban Strategy**
	- **Method:** Questionnaire of Users, External Observers, Qualitative Discussion of Users
	- **Results:**
		- Usability
			- low user-friendliness, needed to ask the chauffeur to do analysis
			- result time took long (not super important)
			- transparency missing
		- Utility
			- The model didn't take all the important factors into account (trainline wasn't included)
		- Usefulness
			- not all experienced the same usefulness
			- Half of the users indicated that usefulness is related to **substance** (learning about the object, more informed outcome), and the other half related it to **process characteristics** (communication, collaboration, efficiency)
			- maybe user involvement in development can help here
	- Nielsen's usability/utility framework is a good starting point but insufficient on its own. Context-dependent factors consistently shaped whether and how the PSS was useful — making it nearly impossible to capture PSS usefulness with a single deterministic model. The authors call for integrating Geertman's (2006) contextual approach with Nielsen's framework going forward
	- Found this Contextual Factors:
	  | Contextual factor | Description |
	  |---|---|
	  | Power relations & hierarchy | Dominant actors (e.g. the area manager) drove PSS interaction, creating very unequal user experiences across participants — something lab settings rarely expose |
	  | Disciplinary background | Users' expertise shapes perceived usefulness; the same tool needs far less explanation with transport/environmental specialists than with urban designers |
	  | Timing in the planning process | Usefulness varies by stage: earlier application yields more learning, later application yields more efficiency gains |
