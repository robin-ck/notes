### Fiscal Tools

Fiscal tools are easy to implement and give decision-makers flexibility to tailor programmes to a community's priorities. They can be piloted at smaller scales before citywide rollout.

| Tool | How it works | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Stormwater Fees/Rates** | Charges based on impervious surface area on a property; funds stormwater management programmes | Raleigh, NC — tiered monthly fee based on impervious area |
| **Stormwater Fee Discounts** | Reduces fees for properties that install BGI or reduce impervious surfaces; performance-based | Washington DC — RiverSmart Rewards: up to 55% discount for BGI that retains stormwater |
| **Stormwater Credits** | Credits on stormwater bills for installing specific BGI practices | Guelph, Canada — credits for peak flow reduction, runoff volume reduction, water quality treatment (max 50%) |
| **Stormwater Credit Trading** | Developers buy off-site credits generated by others installing BGI, as an alternative to on-site compliance | Washington DC — SRC Trading Programme; generators earn revenue, buyers meet stormwater requirements |
| **Grants** | Direct funding to individuals, community groups, and property owners for BGI projects | Tucson — mini-grants up to $45,000 per Ward for neighbourhood stormwater harvesting projects |
| **Rebates & Installation Financing** | Subsidies or financing to offset installation costs; often targeted to flood-prone areas | Hamburg — up to 60% subsidy per m² for green roofs + 50% reduction in rainwater fees |
| **Public–Private Partnerships (PPPs)** | Long-term contracts with private parties to finance and manage BGI at scale | Prince George's County — 30-year Clean Water Partnership to retrofit 4,000 acres of impervious surface |
- ### Non-Fiscal Tools
  
  Non-fiscal tools are voluntary mechanisms. Because they are less intrusive than fiscal tools, they create less resistance and allow policymakers to test programmes before making them mandatory.
  
  | Tool | How it works | Example |
  |---|---|---|
  | **Information & Awareness** | Free consultations, webinars, and outreach to reduce knowledge barriers for property owners | Berlin — Rain Series webinars on decentralised rainwater management for builders and planners |
  | **Fast-Track Project Review** | Planning approvals are expedited for BGI projects, saving developers time and money | Tampa — Green Fast-Track Review moves green projects to the front of the queue |
  | **Pilot & Demonstration Projects** | High-visibility BGI projects that showcase solutions, build community support, and provide education | Antwerp — Stadslab2050 experiments with BGI on rooftops, schools, daycare centres |
  | **Learning Alliances** | Multi-stakeholder groups that share knowledge, coordinate actions, and shape public opinion | Alliance for the Chesapeake Bay — educates Lancaster residents to reduce polluted runoff via BGI |
  | **Mandates** | Building code requirements obliging specific developments to install BGI (e.g. green roofs) | Toronto — Green Roof Bylaw requires 20–60% green roof coverage on new buildings >2,000 m² |
  | **Awards & Recognition** | Competitions and prizes that highlight successful BGI, raising public awareness and encouraging uptake | Barcelona — Green Roofs Competition with 75% subsidy (up to €100,000) for winning projects |
  
  **Key distinction:** Fiscal tools use financial incentives/penalties to shift behaviour; non-fiscal tools use knowledge, recognition, regulation, and process improvements. Cities typically use a mix of both, starting with voluntary non-fiscal tools and progressing to mandates as BGI becomes more mainstream.
